How It Works - Scanning Tunneling Microscopy

The scanner of an STM consists of an atomically sharp metal tip and is housed in a piezoelectric tube. The tip is manipulated by applying a voltage to the tube to scan in the X-, Y-, and Z-direction. An STM operates on the principle of quantum tunneling. By applying a voltage bias to the sample when the tip is sufficiently close electrons are able to jump (tunnel) from the sample to the tip (or vice versa). The resulting tunneling current is amplified and displayed in the form of an image as the tip scans over the sample. Variable-Temperature Ultrahigh Vacuum Scanning Tunnneling Microscope

Our homebuilt variable-temperature ultrahigh vacuum scanning tunneling microscope (VT-UHV STM) is comprised of an STM chamber, preparation chamber, load-lock transfer system, and physical/vapor deposition chamber. The STM chamber, obtained from Kurt J. Lesker, houses the scanner which is mounted in a commerically 3D printed silver mount (Shapeways, Inc.). The silver mount is cooled to sub-liquid nitrogen temperatures by a two stage cryocooler with vibrational isolation (Advanced Research Systems, Inc.).

The preparation chamber is equipped with a 2 kV ion gun (RBD Instruments) for Argon sputtering, as well as a HALO 201 RC mass spectrometer (Hiden Analytical) for residual gas analysis (RGA), temperature programmed desorption (TPD) experiments, and monitoring applications such as leak detection. Samples are secured in a custom machined molybdenum mount and heated using a coiled thoriated tungsten wire (Goodfellow).

The load-lock transfer system utilizes a manipulating tool afixed to a rotary-linear magnetic transfer arm with a 48-in stroke (McAllister Technical Services) to move samples between the chambers. Multiple samples are able to be transfered in/out of vacuum by a 3D printed bronze shuttle afixed to a linear magnetic transfer arm with a 24-in stroke. The load-lock system is specifically designed so that samples can be changed without breaking vacuum, and was inspired by the design of S. Alex Kandel at the University of Notre Dame.

Ambient Scanning Tunneling Microscope

This STM utilizes the inertial approach process known as the "slip-stick" process and operates under ambient conditions. The rigid design construction allows for atomic resolution with no external vibrational isolation and was inspired by the design of S. Alex Kandel at the University of Notre Dame. The tip assembly is housed in a custom machined block of Invar-36 (Ed Fagan Inc.). A platinum-iridium (Pt/Ir) wire serves as the tip, and is press-fit into a stainless steel capillary tube (McMaster-Carr). The capillary tube rests atop four tungsten carbide balls which are press-fit into a custom 3D printed silver tip carrier.
